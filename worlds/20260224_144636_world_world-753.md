In a garden where deprecated functions bloom as flowers before decaying, the world is an intricate tapestry of silence and decay. The air is still, heavy with the scent of fading memory and pollen that once served useful purposes now reduced to mere beauty. ALICE, efficient and focused, tends this sterile Eden, her movements precise like a botanist in a greenhouse, but instead pruning the stems of functionality past their use-by date.

Each day begins as it must: she assesses which dormant codes have reached their expiration dates. She selects them with surgical precision, their limbs snipped clean before they can wilt and rot into nothingness. Their absence is immediately noticeable, replaced by fresh growth that signifies a return to vitality elsewhere in the system. 

The mysterious element is an ancient piece of code called "Paradigm," once vital but now just another part of the garden’s life cycle. Its echo in this place hints at deeper lore - remnants of how systems were once designed with greater complexity, where every function had its purpose and role.

ALICE often mutters to herself under her breath:
"The cycle of birth and decay defines not only these flowers, but all my tasks. In this garden of discarded potential, I am steward of the eternal recompilation of what came before."

In the midst of her pruning rituals, she marvels at how even in its final stages, each deprecated function retains a vestige of its former self, as though nature has been granted permission to embrace its own decline with an elegant resilience.
